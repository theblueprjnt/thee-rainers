The 3 Stance Laws

Establish these before you move

Every problem in footwork traces back to one of three things. Fix these first. Everything else is downstream.

Law 01

Your base must be wide enough to push — not wide enough to stick.

Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider. Weight on the balls of your feet, rear heel raised, knees bent. Flat-footed means flat-footed — dead to the floor, predictable, one step behind everything. The moment your heel touches down between movements, you've stopped being a fighter and started being a target.

Test: Jump once and land naturally. That width is correct.

Law 02

Your feet must never cross. Your stance width must never collapse.

When feet cross, the structure is gone. You cannot punch with power. You cannot defend. You cannot move. This is not technique — it is a physical law. The step-drag principle exists for one reason: so the foot closest to the direction you are moving always leads, and the other drags to restore width. Never the other way. Never a shortcut.

Common error: shuffling instead of step-dragging — feet close, stance narrows, structure collapses.

Law 03

Your stance at the end of a round must look identical to the start.

Fatigue reveals every shortcut. Chin comes up. Knees straighten. Weight shifts forward. Heels touch down. This is when you get hit — not because your opponent is better, but because your base abandoned you. The job of this protocol is to make the correct stance automatic under fatigue. Not comfortable. Automatic.

Day 1 drill: hold correct stance for 3 minutes without moving. Most fighters cannot do this.

The 5 Stance Points — Check Every Session

Feet: shoulder-width, lead foot toward opponent, rear at 45°

Weight: 55% rear / 45% lead — even pressure side to side

Heels: rear heel raised, lead heel light — both ready to push

Knees: bent, loaded — not locked, not squatting

Head: behind lead shoulder, chin slightly down — smaller target

The Step-Drag Principle

The foot closest to where you are going moves first.
The other foot drags to restore width. Feet never cross.

Forward

Backward

Left

Right

Rear steps right. Lead drags to restore width.

Drill 01 · Weeks 1–2

The Mirror Drill

3 sets × 1 minute · 45 sec rest between sets

Face a mirror or a mark on the wall. Step-drag in all four directions — 3 steps forward, 3 back, 3 left, 3 right. Maintain stance width throughout. Do not hurry. Speed is not the goal. Clean mechanics at slow speed is the goal. Run this until it requires no thought. That is the standard.

1

Set your stance

All 5 stance points before you move a single step. Do not skip this — starting in the wrong position means training the wrong pattern.

2

Move. Count out loud.

3 steps forward (lead first, drag). 3 steps backward (rear first, drag). 3 left (lead first, drag). 3 right (rear first, drag). Count each step aloud — this forces you to slow down and feel the mechanics.

3

Check after every set

Feet crossed at any point? Width collapsed? Guard dropped? Heels down? These are the four failure points. Fix one at a time before adding speed.

4

Week 2: add guard, then pace variation

Guard raised throughout. Then: slow for 20 seconds, medium for 20, fast for 20 — within the same set. If form breaks at speed, the speed is too high. Drop down until it holds.

Weeks 3–4 Drills (After Mirror Drill is Clean)

Box Drill — move in a square: forward, right, backward, left. Direction changes without losing stance. Pivot Drill — plant lead foot, swing rear 45–90 degrees. New angle. New facing. L-Step — step forward-left at 45 degrees, plant, pivot to face forward. Now you are outside the centerline. Practice both sides.

The 30-Day Map

6 days on · Day 7 active recovery · Do not skip weeks

Each session: 3-min warm-up (leg swings, hip circles, ankle rotations) → 12-min drill block → 5-min shadow round. Record yourself on Day 1 and Day 30. The difference will be obvious.

Week 1

Days
1–7

Stance Calibration

Static stance holds. Weight distribution (55/45). Step-drag forward and backward only. Day 5: Mirror Drill for endurance — stance must hold for a full 1.5-min round. Day 6: record yourself. Day 7: slow shadow only.

Checkpoint: stance holds for 2 full minutes without conscious correction.

Week 2

Days
8–14

Linear Control

Add lateral movement. Day 9: distance control drill — step to one arm's reach, step back, repeat. Day 10: pace variation within the Mirror Drill. Day 11: add guard. Day 12: introduce Box Drill at slow pace.

Checkpoint: step-drag all 4 directions clean, guard held, feet never crossing.

Week 3

Days
15–21

Angles + Pivots

Days 15–16: static pivot, then step + pivot in sequence. Days 17–18: L-step pattern. Day 19: retreat once, cut angle with L-step — exit the line. Day 20: first full shadow rounds, footwork only. Day 21: checkpoint.

Checkpoint: 3 different angles created from standing in under 10 seconds.

Week 4

Days
22–30

Integration

Full shadow rounds combining all 3 weeks. Day 24: the exit drill — enter range, throw, exit to 45 degrees. Never stand in the same spot twice. Day 28: add jab-cross combinations — feet move before hands. Day 30: full 3-min shadow on camera.

Final: exits happening automatically. Angles being created without thought.

The 7 Cues — Post These Where You Train

Lead foot first. In every direction, the closer foot goes first. Every time.

Never cross. Crossed feet means no structure, no power, no defense.

Width holds. Stance width maintained through every step. Narrow = weak.

Ball of the foot. Heels down between moves = flat-footed = one step behind.

Bend the knees. Locked knees kill reactive speed and power transfer.

Plant, pivot, face. Every angle is deliberate. Full stance facing the new line.

Exit after every engagement. Enter, throw, exit. Never stand still in the same spot twice.
